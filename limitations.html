AI Limitations & Fact-Checking Strategies

Learn to recognise AI limitations, spot hallucinations, and verify information effectively for academic success.

üö® Critical Reality Check

AI tools are powerful learning assistants, but they are not infallible. They can confidently present false information, fabricate sources, and make mathematical errors. Understanding these limitations is essential for using AI responsibly in your academic work.

Never submit work containing AI-generated facts, statistics, or citations without verifying them yourself.

Understanding AI Hallucinations

A "hallucination" occurs when an AI model generates information that sounds plausible but is actually incorrect or entirely fabricated. This is not a bug or malfunction ‚Äì it is an inherent characteristic of how large language models work. They predict likely-sounding text rather than retrieving verified facts.

üî¢ Common Types

  • ‚Ä¢ Fabricated academic sources that do not exist
  • ‚Ä¢ Incorrect statistics or dates
  • ‚Ä¢ Made-up quotes attributed to real people
  • ‚Ä¢ False details about historical events
  • ‚Ä¢ Invented technical specifications
  • ‚Ä¢ Misattributed authorship or publication details

‚ö†Ô∏è Why They Happen

  • ‚Ä¢ Models predict plausible text, not truth
  • ‚Ä¢ Training data contains errors and inconsistencies
  • ‚Ä¢ Models try to please users by providing answers
  • ‚Ä¢ Complex questions push models beyond their knowledge
  • ‚Ä¢ No built-in fact-checking mechanism
  • ‚Ä¢ Statistical patterns can mislead

üéØ When Most Likely

  • ‚Ä¢ Obscure or niche topics with less training data
  • ‚Ä¢ Recent events after the model's knowledge cut-off
  • ‚Ä¢ Requests for specific citations or sources
  • ‚Ä¢ Complex mathematical calculations
  • ‚Ä¢ Detailed technical specifications
  • ‚Ä¢ Questions requiring precise factual knowledge

Essential Fact-Checking Strategies for Students

Develop these habits to verify AI-generated information before using it in your academic work:

Why: AI models can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect numbers.

How:

  • Cross-reference with official sources (government statistics, academic databases)
  • Check multiple independent sources
  • Look for the original data source, not secondary reports
  • Be especially careful with comparative statistics

Example: If AI says "60% of students use AI for homework," find the actual study, check the sample size, date, and methodology before citing it.

Why: AI frequently fabricates citations that sound legitimate but do not exist.

How:

  • Search for the exact paper title in Google Scholar
  • Verify author names and publication years
  • Check if the journal or publisher exists
  • Access the actual source to confirm it says what the AI claims
  • Use university library databases for academic sources

Warning: Never include a citation in your work without verifying the source exists and is relevant. Fabricated references are academic misconduct.

Why: AI can be confidently wrong about technical details, formulas, and specialised knowledge.

How:

  • Check formulas and calculations manually or with specialised software
  • Verify technical specifications against manufacturer documentation
  • Consult authoritative textbooks or academic sources
  • Ask subject matter experts (lecturers, tutors) if uncertain

Tip: For mathematical problems, work through the steps yourself. For scientific facts, check recent peer-reviewed papers.

Why: Different models have different training data and may catch each other's errors.

How:

  • Ask the same question to ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini
  • Look for consensus across multiple models
  • Investigate any discrepancies thoroughly
  • Use specialised tools (Perplexity with citations, NotebookLM for your documents)

Strategy: If models disagree, that is a red flag to do additional research using academic databases.

Why: Models cannot know about events after their training data cut-off unless they have web access.

How:

  • Know your AI tool's knowledge cut-off date
  • Use tools with web search for recent information
  • Verify any "current" information with news sources or official websites
  • Be cautious with evolving fields (technology, medicine, current events)

Current Models (2025): ChatGPT (GPT-5) has October 2024 cut-off, Claude Sonnet 4.5 has April 2024 cut-off, Gemini 2.5 Pro has early 2025 cut-off. Always check the latest information for your specific tool as these dates update regularly.

Recommended Verification Tools

Academic Databases

  • Google Scholar: Free, comprehensive academic search
  • Web of Science: Citation tracking and verification
  • PubMed: Medical and life sciences
  • JSTOR: Humanities and social sciences
  • IEEE Xplore: Engineering and technology

Fact-Checking Tools

  • Perplexity AI: Provides citations with responses
  • Consensus: Academic research search with citations
  • Semantic Scholar: AI-powered research tool with TLDRs
  • Connected Papers: Visual paper exploration

Official Sources

  • ONS: UK official statistics
  • WHO: Health information
  • Gov.uk: UK government data
  • Eurostat: European statistics
  • UN Data: International statistics

Understanding Context Windows & Token Limits

The context window (also called context length) is the maximum amount of text an AI model can process at once. This includes your entire conversation history, uploaded documents, and the model's responses.

What Counts Towards the Limit

  • ‚Ä¢ All previous messages in the conversation
  • ‚Ä¢ All AI responses you have received
  • ‚Ä¢ Any uploaded documents or files
  • ‚Ä¢ System instructions (invisible to you)
  • ‚Ä¢ The new message you are sending

What Happens at the Limit

  • ‚Ä¢ Oldest messages may be "forgotten"
  • ‚Ä¢ Context from early conversation is lost
  • ‚Ä¢ You may need to start a new conversation
  • ‚Ä¢ Uploaded documents might be truncated
  • ‚Ä¢ Some tools show warnings before reaching limits

Current Context Windows (2025)

ChatGPT (GPT-5): 128,000 tokens

Approximately 96,000 words or 300 pages

Claude Sonnet 4.5: 200,000 tokens

Approximately 150,000 words or 500 pages

Gemini 2.5 Pro: 1,000,000+ tokens

Approximately 750,000+ words or 2,000+ pages

Gemini 2.5 Flash: 1,000,000+ tokens

Faster responses with same large context

Note: Actual usable context may be less due to system instructions and other overhead. Context windows are regularly expanding, so check the latest model documentation.

Best Practices for Managing Context

‚úÖ Do These

  • ‚Ä¢ Start new conversations for different topics
  • ‚Ä¢ Summarise key points periodically in long conversations
  • ‚Ä¢ Upload only relevant sections of long documents
  • ‚Ä¢ Be concise in your prompts when possible
  • ‚Ä¢ Use tools with larger context for document analysis
  • ‚Ä¢ Save important responses outside the conversation

‚ùå Avoid These

  • ‚Ä¢ Keeping one conversation going for weeks
  • ‚Ä¢ Uploading entire textbooks when you need one chapter
  • ‚Ä¢ Assuming the model remembers everything from the start
  • ‚Ä¢ Mixing multiple unrelated topics in one conversation
  • ‚Ä¢ Ignoring context limit warnings
  • ‚Ä¢ Relying on very old context for current questions

üéØ The Golden Rule of AI Verification

If you cannot verify it, do not cite it. If you cannot explain it, you do not understand it.

Your lecturers expect you to understand and be able to defend every claim in your work. AI should enhance your learning and research process, not replace your critical thinking and verification responsibilities.
